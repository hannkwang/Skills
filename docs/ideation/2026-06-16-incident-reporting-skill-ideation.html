Incident-Management Reporting Skill

Directions worth exploring for a skill that, during a live incident, drafts the immediate WhatsApp group message, the senior-stakeholder email, and the phone-call brief to update PS — from what is known at the time.

Date 2026-06-16 Topic incident-reporting-skill Focus multi-channel incident reporting (WhatsApp / email / call) Mode repo-grounded

Grounding Context

Codebase context. This repo is a small collection of Singapore-public-service security & compliance Claude skills, each a single self-contained *-SKILL.md with YAML frontmatter (name, description) plus reference files. Two ship today: risk-assessment-writer and im8-applicability-check. The established house conventions ideation should reuse rather than reinvent:

  • BLUF / decision-first. Every output leads with the decision or status in one or two lines, "no throat-clearing" (risk-assessment-writer, core principle 1).
  • Guided batch intake. Skills extract what the user already said, then ask for the rest in one consolidated message, marking gaps [TBC] and listing all TBCs at the end — never one-question-at-a-time interrogation.
  • Required-inputs tables & fixed output structures. Both skills pin an exact section order and a self-check pass before presenting.
  • Severity / risk vocabulary. risk-assessment-writer carries a 5×5 likelihood×impact matrix and a mitigations catalog; the incident example already reasons in this language ("Risk assessed as low", "Incident severity: Medium per SN incident guidelines").
  • SG-gov domain furniture in the worked example. SITREP, GIROC, PS/DS, ACISO, classification, WPD/HR coordination, and a recurrence signal ("second SN PKI card loss in two months").

The worked WhatsApp example supplied at invocation is the primary evidence artifact. It has a stable shape: a bracketed header line [Incident] [date time] [title], then numbered items grouped under Incident Details → Actions Taken → (embedded) Risk & Severity → Proposed Actions → Next steps. External web research was not run — this is an internal SG-gov reporting format with no useful public prior art to scan beyond the cross-domain analogies cited per idea.

Topic Axes

The subject was decomposed into five orthogonal surfaces; each idea below targets one.

  • Channel formats — the three artifacts as distinct shapes: WhatsApp (short, scannable), email (fuller, on-record), phone brief (spoken, seconds).
  • Information intake — capturing and structuring what is known at the time of an incident.
  • Accuracy & safety guardrails — honesty about unknowns, consistency across the three artifacts, classification/channel sensitivity.
  • Severity & escalation routing — deciding severity and who must be told (PS / DS / SITREP / the group) through which channel.
  • Progressive updates — first-alert → follow-ups → closure as the incident evolves.

1 One incident record → three consistent renders

axis · channel formats confidence 90% complexity Medium

Capture the incident once into a single canonical structured record (the facts, timeline, actions, risk, severity, proposed actions), then mechanically project it into all three artifacts — WhatsApp, email, phone brief — in one pass. The three outputs are views of one source, not three independently authored drafts.

Basis direct: The supplied example already is a single fact set rendered in WhatsApp shape; the email and call simply re-cut the same numbered items (Incident Details, Actions Taken, Risk/Severity, Proposed Actions). The repo's two skills both pin "a fixed output structure from structured inputs," so a record-first design is the house pattern, not a new one.
Rationale Under incident time pressure the expensive, error-prone work is re-keying the same facts into three formats and keeping them consistent — the exact failure where the WhatsApp says "low" and the email says "medium." Make consistency structural rather than a discipline the reporter has to maintain at 2am.
Downsides Requires defining the canonical schema up front; if the three channel templates diverge in what they must contain, the projection logic carries that complexity. Over-templating could make outputs feel robotic for nuanced incidents.

2 Guided batch intake (blank-page killer)

axis · information intake confidence 88% complexity Low

Don't ask the reporter to write — ask them structured questions. A required-inputs checklist (what/who, when discovered, when reported, actions taken, risk read, severity, proposed actions, audience) extracted first from whatever they already pasted, then the gaps asked in one batch, with anything still open marked [TBC] and listed at the end. The skill writes; the human supplies facts.

Basis direct: Both shipping skills implement exactly this — "First, extract what the user has already told you… then ask the rest in ONE batch" (im8-applicability) and the Required-inputs table + "mark unknowns [TBC]" pattern (risk-assessment-writer).
Rationale During an incident the reporter is rattled and time-poor; a blank "describe the incident" prompt yields slow, uneven output. A checklist guarantees the non-negotiable fields (discovery time vs report time, police-report status, account disabled y/n) are never silently dropped — the things senior management asks about first.
Downsides A rigid checklist can feel like friction for a trivial incident; needs a fast-path for when the reporter pastes a near-complete narrative. Must resist asking questions whose answers were already given.

3 Confidence-tagged facts & an explicit "not yet known" line

axis · accuracy & safety guardrails confidence 85% complexity Low

Tag each captured fact as confirmed, reported, or unconfirmed, and carry those tags through every artifact. Each output ends with a short "What we don't yet know" line so senior management can see the edge of current knowledge instead of inferring false certainty. This is the operational meaning of the user's own framing — "based on what is known at the time." It also absorbs a classification guardrail: keep sensitive detail off WhatsApp and point to a secure follow-up.

Basis direct: The example mixes confidence levels silently — "No suspicious login activity detected, ongoing monitoring in place" (an evolving observation) sits beside hard facts like "account and PKI card disabled immediately." reasoned: Senior management acts on these messages within minutes; an unmarked unconfirmed fact that later flips is the most damaging failure mode of fast reporting, so the skill should make confidence first-class.
Rationale Reframes "what is known at the time" from a caveat the reporter remembers to add, into a property the skill enforces — and gives the next update (idea 6) a precise list of what to chase: the items still tagged unconfirmed.
Downsides Over-tagging can clutter a message meant to be scannable; the rendering must keep tags lightweight (e.g. only flag unconfirmed items, treat confirmed as the default). Reporters may guess at confidence — needs gentle prompting, not enforcement.

4 Phone-to-PS as a spoken SBAR brief with a Q&A card

axis · channel formats confidence 80% complexity Low

The phone update is not a document read aloud — it is a 30-second spoken brief plus an anticipated-questions card. Render it in SBAR order (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation), written for the ear: short sentences, the ask stated plainly, and 3–4 likely PS questions ("Is it contained? Is data exposed? Who else knows? What do you need from me?") with one-line answers ready.

Basis external: SBAR is the standard verbal-handoff structure in clinical and emergency-response settings precisely for high-stakes, time-boxed spoken updates — a well-matched analogy for briefing a Permanent Secretary by phone. direct: The example's numbered prose is unreadable aloud; a caller needs a different shape than the WhatsApp text.
Rationale The phone call is the most pressured of the three channels and the one the reporter is least equipped to improvise. A spoken script plus a Q&A card directly de-risks the moment most likely to go badly, and SBAR's "Recommendation" slot forces a clear ask rather than a status dump.
Downsides Spoken style diverges from the written artifacts, so it's the one render that isn't a clean projection of the record — adds template surface. Anticipated questions are heuristic and may miss what a given PS actually asks.

5 Severity classifier & escalation-routing recommendation

axis · severity & escalation routing confidence 78% complexity Medium

From the captured facts, propose a severity rating against the SN incident guidelines and, from that, a routing recommendation: which audiences must be told (group / PS / DS / SITREP), through which channel, and by when. The output is advisory — it surfaces the call and its reasoning so the reporter confirms rather than guesses under pressure.

Basis direct: The example already carries both a severity verdict ("Incident severity: Medium per SN incident guidelines") and routing decisions ("Incident to be reported to SITREP on 11 Jan evening"; "incident report update to PS and DS"). reasoned: The repo's risk-assessment-writer shows the house already trusts matrix-anchored severity calls, so encoding the SN severity bands is consistent with existing practice.
Rationale Mis-rating severity or missing a required notification is a governance failure, not a style miss. Making the skill propose-and-justify (with the reporter as the approving check) catches the omission while keeping a human accountable for the call.
Downsides Depends on the actual SN severity bands and notification thresholds, which must be supplied (likely [TBC] until provided) — the skill should not invent them. Routing rules may differ by incident type and evolve.

6 Incident lifecycle with delta-only follow-ups

axis · progressive updates confidence 76% complexity Medium

Treat an incident as a short-lived stateful object with explicit statuses — First alert → Ongoing → Contained → Closed — and generate updates accordingly. A "first alert" mode reports honestly when almost nothing is confirmed yet but commits a next-update time; follow-ups render only the delta since the last message, so senior management reads what changed rather than re-reading the whole timeline.

Basis direct: The example closes with "Next steps: No further updates required" — a status signal that presumes a lifecycle the skill could make explicit. external: Military / emergency-ops SITREPs (which the domain already uses) are inherently serial status updates with stated states, and the "report only what changed" convention is standard practice for recurring command updates.
Rationale The user explicitly needs reporting "within minutes" and again as things evolve. Pairs tightly with idea 3: the delta is computed from which unconfirmed facts became confirmed and which actions completed since the last message.
Downsides Requires persisting prior-update state across turns, which is more than a one-shot draft; for short incidents the lifecycle may be overkill. A pure-delta message can lose context for a recipient who missed the prior one — may need a "since first reported" recap toggle.

7 Recurrence detection feeding Proposed Actions

axis · severity & escalation routing confidence 72% complexity Medium

When the reporter (or a lightweight incident log) indicates this is not the first of its kind, the skill flags the pattern — "Nth similar incident in X months" — and uses it to seed the Proposed Actions section with systemic measures, distinct from the per-incident response. This is the bridge from immediate reporting toward the downstream formal report to PS/DS and potentially the existing risk-assessment-writer skill.

Basis direct: The example's items 9–11 are exactly this move — "This is the second SN PKI card loss in two months by different officers, indicating need for enhanced measures" → a concrete systemic proposal (mass email, storage guidelines, awareness training) → an owner and a downstream report to PS and DS.
Rationale The Proposed Actions section is where an incident report stops being a log and starts driving change; recurrence is the single strongest trigger for it. Captures compounding value — one incident capture feeds the immediate messages, the SITREP entry, and the systemic case — rather than throwing the record away after the WhatsApp send.
Downsides True recurrence detection needs an incident history the skill may not have; absent a log, it relies on the reporter knowing and saying so. Over-eagerly proposing systemic measures on a one-off could read as alarmist — the flag should be evidence-gated, not default-on.
Backbone. Ideas 1–3 form the core the rest hang off: a single record (1), filled by guided intake (2), with confidence-tagged facts (3). Ideas 4–7 are higher-value extensions — spoken-channel handling, severity routing, lifecycle updates, and recurrence — that all read from that same record. A first version could ship the backbone alone and still be useful.
